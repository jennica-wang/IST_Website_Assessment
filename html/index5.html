Chemical Reactions

A chemical reaction occurs when one or more substances (reactants) form one or more new substances (products). When a chemical change occurs, the following may occur:
  • A change in colour
  • A precipitate (solid) forms
  • An energy is produced (e.g. fizzing or burning)
  • An odour is produced

A chemical equation is written using chemical formulas. The reactants are on the left and the products are on the right. The two are separated by an arrow symbol. Each individual substance's chemical formula is separated from each other by a plus sign. State symbols indicate the state of matter of each compound or molecule. They are placed in subscript next to the combound by an appreviation in parentheses. They are:

  • Solid (s)
  • Liquid (l)
  • Gas (g)
  • Aqueous (aq)

Aqueous means dissolved in water.

The Law of Conservation of Matter

Matter can change form through physical and chemical changes, but through any of these changes, matter is conserved. The total mass of the products remains equal to the total mass of the reactants. This concept is called the Law of Conservation of Matter. This means that the same number and kind of atoms are present before and after the change.

Salt, Solubility and Solutions

Term Definition
Solvent A liquid that a substance dissolves in (usually water)
Solute The substance that is dissolved
Solution The mixture of solvent and solute
Soluble substance A substance which can dissolve
Insoluble substance A substance which can not dissolve
Salt An ionic compound
Salt solution Soluble salt dissolved in water

Precipitation Reactions

If two salt solutions are mixed, two results are generally possible:
  1. Nothing happens - the ions in the solution remain free and no new compounds are formed.
  2. A reaction occurs - some of the ions join together to form a new compound that is insoluble.

The substance formed is known as the precipitate, and the chemical reaction is known as a precipitation reaction. When two salt solutions are mixed, new salt combinations are possible. The positive metal ion is one salt solution combines with the negative ion in the other and vice versa. If one of these salts are insoluble, then it will precipitate from the solution.

The general formula for a precipitation reaction is:
AB(aq) + CD(aq) ➜ CB(s) + AD(aq)
