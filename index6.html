Periodic Trends

Acids

Acids are compounds which separate in water to form hydrogen ions, H+ (they “ionise”). How strong an acid solution is, has to do with the number of (concentration) H+ ions in the solution. The greater the H+ concentration, the stronger the acid. Example: HCl (hydrochloric acid) in water.

Examples: Hydrochloric acid (found in stomach acid), vinegar, citrus juice, coffee/tea, carbonated soft drinks.

Bases

Bases substances which neutralise acids. They are usually ionic compounds, metal hydroxides, that separate in water to form a negatively charged hydroxide ion (OH-) and positively charged metal ion. The strength of a base is determined by the concentration of hydroxide ions (OH-). The greater the concentration of OH- ions the stronger the base.

Examples: NaOH (sodium hydroxide) in water, ammonia, bleach, soap, toothpaste, cleaning agents.

Alkalis

Water soluble bases are called alkalis. An alkali is a water-soluble base. All alkalis are bases, but not all bases are alkalis!
